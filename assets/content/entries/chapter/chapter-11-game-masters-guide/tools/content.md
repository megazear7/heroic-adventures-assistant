Game Master’s Manual

One player will take on the title of game master. The game master’s primary responsibility is to create the setting, populate it with creatures and non-playable characters, and present this world to the players. The game master will play as all the creatures in the world that are not the player characters and will arbitrate the rules if there are any questions. It is suggested that the most experienced player is the game master.

Heroic Adventures is designed to be an easy to play pen and paper roleplaying game. While experienced game masters will have their tried-and-true methods, they along with inexperienced game masters might be looking for advice when it comes to this specific game. This chapter will go over some details of game mastering that are specific to Heroic Adventures and then share some tips for running a pen and paper roleplaying game.

It is also important to remember that everything in this chapter is a suggestion and should be modified as you see fit. Use your own judgement when creating encounters that you think will be easy or difficult, when determining how much treasure to give, and so forth. You are encouraged to come up with your own monsters, abilities, and even spells as you see fit.

## Combat Encounters

The game master will need to come up with combat encounters for the players to participate in. Each monster will have a given level. When determining the difficulty of a combat encounter, add up the total level of the characters in the combat encounter and the total level of the monsters.

### Prepare for Encounters

It is important to have at the ready all of the player initiative cards that will be needed in the encounter. After character creation, level advancement, or switching weapons, ask the player’s what their initiatives are and have those cards at the ready. Also, have prepared the initiative cards that you expect you will need based on the monsters you expect the character’s will be fighting. You can pre-shuffle this deck and spend a minimal amount of time swapping cards in and out before encounters in order to keep the game moving.

### Easy Encounters

Easy encounters include a total level of the monsters within the encounter to be less than twice as low as the total level of the characters participating in that combat encounter. In this case, the characters should sustain very little damage and should not be challenged.

### Standard Encounters

Standard encounters would include a total level of the monsters within the encounter to be equal to or less than the total level of the characters participating in that combat encounter and more than half of the total level of the characters. In this case, the characters should sustain some damage but be able to confidently overcome the enemy creatures.

### Difficult Encounters

Difficult encounters would include a total level of the monsters within the encounter to be above the total level of the characters and up to twice the total level of the characters. These encounters would significantly drain the characters resources including health, consumables, mana, and heroic actions.

### Diversify Your Encounters

Each character has strengths and weaknesses. Each encounter should in some way target the weaknesses of the characters. This may mean placing monsters in the encounter that target willpower instead of agility, have high initiative to overcome bonus attacks, or cast magic missiles to overcome a high toughness.

### Flow Your Encounters

Each encounter should flow in one way or another into the next encounter. Given the initiative system used in Heroic Adventures, it is very easy to go from one encounter to the next with no setup time. This means that the time between encounters can be quick.

It is advisable to have the enemies that the characters are fighting to push the action as much as the players push the action. Instead of sitting back and waiting for the players to move forward, the enemies might make an attack of their own. In this way a dungeon crawl, or any adventure, can be a push and pull between the characters and their enemies, forcing the action upon each other and keeping the game moving fast.

### Challenge the Players

Make sure that your players cannot defeat every encounter. Make it clear that they need to constantly be on their toes and know when enough is enough, when they are in over their head and need to plan their escape. This play style means that no encounter is routine, and any encounter could escalate into something that they need to escape.

### Escalate Your Encounters

Most encounters should not end the same way they began. Introduce environmental effects that change the terrain. Send in reinforcements for the monsters that might make an easy encounter into a big challenge. The reinforcements could come from different directions, be equipped to target characters who thought they were untouchable or well protected.

### Equipment by Level

By level 7 the players should have enough coins to purchase an artisan item such as a weapon, armor, or shield. By level 13 the players should have enough coins to purchase a mastercrafted item.

### Putting the Pressure On

As the players adventure, they will need to rest and recover before engaging themselves in more dangerous situations. Whether they are resting at an inn or leaving a dungeon to go back to town, these are opportunities for you as the game master to force the players into some tough situations.

When the player characters are vulnerable because of spent mana, low health, and lacking consumables such as health potions and ward stones, you can throw them into an encounter that they were not expecting. Old npcs that they did not treat well might be after them, or whatever enemies they were facing in their adventures might be bringing the action to the players, or some unexpected situation may arise such as the town being attacked by outside forces.

In these situations, the players need to know when to retreat. Sometimes retreating is not an option, and this gives exciting moments to a campaign where the players have to step up and truly take risks in order to save themselves.

### Draining Party Resources

As an adventure unfolds, consider ways to spend party resources before they get to the main encounters that you have planned. If they are searching for an evil wizard, throw some smaller encounters at them first that might require them to spend mana, potions, and ward stones. In this way you make sure that they do not go into every encounter at full strength but must deal with situations where they have been depleted of resources.

### Balancing the Power of Magic

As magic users increase in level, particular wizards, warlocks, druids, and other characters that have access to a lot of mana and spells, it is important to balance the power of these characters with other characters that do not have access to spells. There are a few ways that you can handle this as a game master.

First, do not allow the magic using classes to fight in one encounter, rest up to regain mana, and then fight in one more encounter, and so on. Instead, force situations that require the magic using classes to be careful with how much mana they spend. They might know for example that they are in a dangerous place and so they need to preserve mana and not use it on the first few encounters. Alternatively, when the spell casting characters are low on mana, such as when they are deep in a dungeon after many encounters of combat, getting out of the dungeon requires fighting through more enemies. With the mana depleted, these last few encounters offer opportunities for other characters to shine.

Secondly, consider giving enemies more ward runes to protect them against spell casting characters. Goblins might have ward runes to protect them from spell casting elves, and a necromancer might place ward runes on his skeleton horde. In this way you can dampen the damage output of spell casting classes which allows for other character classes to be more involved.

Alternatively, if you feel that the spell casting characters are not involved enough in the combat and those players are feeling bored, do not give monsters ward runes and allow those players to have more opportunities to affect the combat.

## Creating a Setting

Focus on creating a setting rather than predetermining a story. Allow the players to travel through a world, encounter non playable characters that you have outlined, and face dangers that hide rewards. Also, do not predetermine everything about the setting that you are creating. Allow the players to make some determinations without even knowing it.

You might spend a lot of time outlining the details to a dungeon and the players might decide to go in a completely different direction. This is okay, because you can use the dungeon you created regardless of what ancient door or gaping cave the players decide to walk into. If you need to adjust your setting after the game in order to align with what happened, that means that you are giving players freedom to make their own choices. This is not only expected, but desirable. The same rule goes for non-playable characters.

A setting involves locations and characters. Locations have descriptions and characters have motivations. Once these two things are in place, your setting is ready.

### Creating Non-Playable Characters

When creating non playable characters (npcs), focus on describing their motivations. What do they want and what is their plan for getting it? Make sure that different non playable characters have competing or mutually exclusive motivations. One might want to overthrow the king, and another might want to remain as king. Another might want to overthrow the entire kingdom. Or, on a much smaller scale, a character might want to build a cabin with a small farm but a tribe in the woods wants the woodland to remain as hunting grounds.

As the players interact with these non-playable characters, keep their motivations in mind and allow the npcs to respond to the actions of the players by trying to achieve their goals. This might be staying safe, using the players to overthrow an enemy, and so on. In this way the players will feel like they are interacting with real characters in a developing story that they are helping to create.

### NPCs and Proteges

As you develop the world that the players have adventured in each session, it is important to encourage players to develop relationships to some NPC’s. It might be a local villager who sent them to clear out some goblins from a forest, an innkeeper who they often visit, or any npc that they repeatedly encounter. One way to encourage this is to explain to the player’s that NPCs can become their protégés. In this way, players will be more likely to roleplay and look for opportunities to trust and work with NPCs.

When a player has developed a sufficient relationship with an NPC, allow the player to roll up stats for that NPC and turn them into their own characters protégé. In this way the players can contribute to the game world and play characters that exist in the world, have a history, purpose, and life within the world prior to the player playing them as a character.

### Character Professions

Characters will often need time to rest and recover from their adventures. During this time, the characters will need a place to stay, which can cost them coin. Inns usually cost one coin per character per night. When travelling through unknown lands or in places they otherwise have not been before, this small cost can add up.

When characters are at their hometown or in a place that they have otherwise come to call home, encourage them to use their expertise such as blacksmithing, fishing, or sailing to gain money while not adventuring. This should be a number of coins per day equal to their expertise in the chosen skill. This can cover the costs of an inn but should also require some roleplaying on the part of the players to explain what they are doing to make this coin. This also means that when the players decide to go places, they have never been before or travel abroad, they lose access to this steady stream of income.

Additionally, as the game master, you could introduce NPCs that threaten this stream of income, giving the players motivation to take action.

### Character Retirement

When characters reach level 10, have some epic concluding adventure planned for the players. This could be some adventure that is the culmination of many sessions of play and could dramatically affect the state of the world that the characters are in. After this concluding adventure, the characters that have now reached max level can retire in some way that fits each character.

A wizard might build a tower and live within it studying magic. A fighter might build a training compound where he trains recruits who he leads as a mercenary band. A soldier might become the general to the king. An Alchemist might spend his days creating powerful magical weapons that become legendary artifacts of the game world. Work with each player based upon their character’s role within the game world to determine how they retire.

Any protégé’s of those characters should retire with the main character, allowing the players to enter back into this world as level 1 characters. Their old characters are now key npcs of the world in which they play.

## Creating Locations

When describing locations, provide plenty of details that might interest the players, but be vague, or at least flexible, about the specifics such as the location within the world. If an invading enemy has set up a camp and you want the players to encounter this camp, allow the players to choose where they want to go and then put the camp in the game world wherever it needs to be so that the players encounter it. Give the players hints to act on, and then wherever they go the enemy camp happens to be at that place. The players feel like they followed the clues and discovered it, but their free choice did not ruin the time you spent preparing the adventure. After the game you can update your notes as needed.

Include monster profiles within locations with some sample encounters that you can quickly pull up to throw at the players based upon the actions they take. Have a few easy encounters and moderate encounters in mind such as scouting parties, unprepared encampments of the enemy, guards at an entrance, and the like. Also have in mind some no-win scenario encounters that you can throw at the players. This is a way to drive the players away in order to provide an up and down rhythm to the story and force the players to realize that not every fight is a fight worth having. You could also place some monsters that are far too powerful for the players throughout certain locations, such as a dragon in a cave system that is filled with otherwise easy to defeat monsters. The players will again need to stop and think about which fights are worth having and which need to be avoided.

### Repeat Locations

After creating locations, come up with reasons for why the characters may want to return to the same location multiple times. This allows the players feel like they are getting to know the game world and are not just strung along from one place to the next.

Some ways to do this is to have an inn that they keep coming back to that has familiar names and faces. This inn might have gambling that the characters can participate in or a fight ring that the characters can compete at. There could also be a dungeon complex with multiple competing factions that is a recurring source of problems for the locals, or a recurring opportunity for treasure. Also, when introducing new threats and opportunities for the players to act on, consider involving past locations
