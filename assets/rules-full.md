# Heroic Adventures

*- An Adventure Chronicles Game -*

#### Introduction

*Heroic Adventures* is a pen and paper roleplaying game that mixes modern mechanics, fast paced combat, and deep role play potential. From card-based initiative to opposed attack roles, this is a new take on tabletop gaming with a classic feel. The rules encourage a dynamically evolving storyline and tactical combat.

Character creation is simple, and characters remain easy to play even at higher levels. At the same time, the simple rules come together in an elegant mixture that requires preparation and strategy to succeed. Additionally, the outcomes of combat and the preparation for adventure will require players to contribute to the arc of the naturally developing stories.

#### The Game Master and the Players

One person takes on the role of “game master” while everyone else is referred to as “players.” The game master will prepare for each session of the game by creating an adventure that includes locations, other characters, and various monsters and challenges. In the first session the players will create characters, and the game master will introduce the game world that the players will be playing in.

The main aspect of every character is their race, such as human, dwarf, elf, and so forth along with a class, such as a fighter, wizard, druid, and so on. These two choices along with a set of number-based attributes and features will define who a character is and what they can do.

#### Game Sessions

In each session the game master will describe the world that the players are in, and the players will take actions based upon that description. This back-and-forth dialog between the players and the game master is called roleplaying. Sometimes the players actions will put them in conflict with the characters and monsters of the world that the game master has created. When this happens, an encounter is played. Miniatures are placed on a battle mat, dice are rolled, and fate will have its way.

A campaign is a series of sessions, maybe played on a weekly or monthly basis on “game night”, wherein everyone gathers to play the game. Over the course of a campaign players level up their characters, a story unfolds in the world that the game master and players have both helped to create, and characters might even meet their doom.

#### The Story Continues Beyond Death

When a character dies, usually during a tough encounter, the player creates a new character. Most likely, however, the player will have a protégé, which is a lower-level character that they have ready and lined up to play. A player may create a protégé when their main character reaches third level. Any time their main character levels up, their protégé levels up. In this way they always have a new character to play that is close enough in level to the rest of the group that they will not be severely disadvantaged. Also, their new character can continue the story where their main character left off.

# <img src="assets/images/media/image6.png" style="width:8.73949in;height:4.36974in" />Feats

Instead of taking race or class features, you may select a feature from the list of feats. These are features that any race or character class can pick from.

### Footwork: Feature

Increase your minor movement speed by 1.

### Swift: Feature

Increase your major movement speed by 1.

### Nimble: Feature

Make a minor movement on the enemy minor action card, before the enemy creatures move.

### Tough: Feature

Increase your health by 4. Requires level 3+.

### Hardy: Feature

Increase your health by 4. Requires Tough and level 9+.

### Learned: Feature

Gain training in 3 different expertise. Reduce the level requirements for all expertise by 3.

### Weapon Training: Feature

Gain training in 2 different weapon categories. Reduce level requirements for all weapon training by 3.

### Ranged Combat Specialist: Feature

When making a ranged attack against a target that is adjacent to a single ally, that ally does not need to make an agility defense roll if you miss your attack.

### Close Range Fighting: Feature

When you make a ranged attack, you may shoot through allies that are not adjacent to the target.

### Dual Wielding: Bonus Action

You may wield two weapons and attack with both weapons when making a bonus attack. Requires level 5+.

### Scholar: Feature

You may spend 1 hour to make a roll on the mages guild table without being at the mage’s guild and modify the result by 1.

### Scroll Master: Feature

You may modify the result of a mage’s guild roll up or down by 3. Requires **Scholar** and intelligence 5+.

### Craftsmen: Feature

When purchasing an artisan or mastercrafted item, spend 1d20 days crafting it instead. The total cost is reduced by 75 for artisan items and 300 for mastercrafted items.

### Quick Draw: Feature

You may switch weapon profiles with a minor action. If you already took a major action this round you cannot do so again even if your new initiative card is drawn.

### Far Shot: Feature

Double the range of all ranged weapons.

### Cavalier: Feature

While mounted, gain +2 initiative and advantage on charge attacks.

### Cook: Feature

Spend 5 coins to purchase from a market and provide a meal for the entire party. Double the healing everyone receives from resting that day.

### Alchemist: Feature

You may spend eather to craft items normally available only from the alchemist's guild. Each eather spent requires 1 hour of work. Requires 5 eather to acquire this feat.

### Valor: Feature

Gain 1 bonus heroic action per day if your background does not already provide one. Requires level 5+.

### Unyielding: Feature

Increase your toughness by 1. Requires level 5+.

### Vampirism: Feature

When you score a critical hit, drain life from a creature you hit with that melee attack: that creature takes an extra 1d6 damage, and you regain health equal to that additional damage dealt. You take double damage from divine spells and take 1d6 damage from sunlight at the start of each turn that you are exposed. Requires 5 scrolls to acquire this feat. Cannot take Lycanthropy.

### Lycanthropy: Feature

Spend a heroic action to transform into a werewolf for the rest of the encounter. Additionally, each time the sun sets, roll a d20. On a roll of a 1 you transform into a werewolf and lose control over your characters actions. While in your werewolf form, you have the stats of a “slayer” monster of your level. Requires 5 scrolls to acquire this feat. Cannot take Vampirism.

### Possessed: Feature

A demon inhabits your body: gain +1 to intelligence and -2 willpower. You must spend 5 eather to acquire this feat. Cannot take Blessed.

### Blessed: Feature

Divine favor guides you: gain +1 to willpower rolls and increase your block by 1 against undead and demons. As a heroic action, allow a character to reroll any dice roll of a 1. Requires 5 eather to acquire this feat. Cannot take Possessed.

### Venom Resistance: Feature

Gain advantage on checks against poison and halve damage from poison effects. Requires 5 ether to acquire this feat.

### Darkvision: Feature

You can see in dim light as if it were bright light and in darkness as if it were dim light, up to 60 spaces. Requires 5 ether to acquire this feat.

### Survivalist: Feature

You regain the normal amount of health when resting in the wild as if you were at an inn or other resting place.

### Double Draft: Feature

You may use two consumables on a minor action or a major action instead of one.

### Cross Training: Feature

You may take a class feature from another class. Requires level 5+ and you must spend 5 eather.

### Multi Class: Feature

You may take a class feature from another class. Requires cross training, level 13+, and you must spend 5 eather.

### Banishment: Feature

Use your willpower for attack and damage against demons. Requires Cleric, Priest, or Paladin.

## Skill

##### Picking Locks (*skill*)

Picking Locks is the art of bypassing mechanical and magical barriers, from rusty dungeon padlocks to enchanted vault doors. Skilled lockpickers use deft fingers and specialized tools—like thieves’ picks or enchanted keys—to disable tumblers or unravel wards. In a world of hidden treasures and monster-guarded secrets, this expertise opens paths to forbidden chambers or ancient crypts. Yet, failure risks triggering traps or alerting foes, making it a gamble of precision and nerve.

##### Picking Pockets (*skill*)

Picking Pockets is the subtle craft of lifting valuables from unsuspecting targets, whether in bustling marketplaces or shadowy tavern corners. It demands agility and stealth, as a deft hand slips into pouches or cloaks to snatch coins, keys, or enchanted trinkets. In a realm of magic, skilled pickpockets might pilfer spell components or cursed artifacts, risking detection by vigilant guards or mystical alarms. This expertise thrives in daring heists or covert missions where wealth and secrets change hands unseen.

##### Setting Traps (*skill*)

Setting Traps is the expertise of crafting snares, pitfalls, and ambushes to ensnare foes or secure lairs. It blends mechanical ingenuity with magical enhancements, from tripwires and pressure plates to enchanted glyphs that freeze or burn intruders. In a world teeming with monsters, traps can guard camps, slow pursuers, or weaken beasts before a fight. This skill demands foresight and cunning, turning the environment into a weapon against the unwary.

##### Sleight of Hand (*skill*)

Sleight of Hand is the mastery of quick, deceptive movements, blending physical dexterity with minor magical illusions. It’s used to palm objects, perform tricks, or conceal small items like daggers or spell scrolls. In a fantasy setting, it might involve vanishing coins, swapping potions, or subtly casting cantrips unnoticed. This expertise shines for rogues, performers, and enchanters who rely on misdirection to outwit foes or dazzle crowds.

##### Fishing (*skill*)

Fishing is the skill of catching aquatic creatures, from mundane fish to magical beasts lurking in enchanted waters. It involves knowledge of bait, tackle, and the habits of elusive prey like merfolk or sea serpents. In a world of adventure, fishing might yield rare ingredients for potions or clues to sunken treasures. This expertise suits patient hunters or seafaring explorers seeking sustenance or secrets from the deep.

##### Healing (*skill*)

Healing is the vital art of mending wounds and curing ailments, blending mundane medicine with magical restoration. Skilled healers use bandages, herbs, and potions alongside spells or divine blessings to close gashes, purge poisons, or revive the fallen. In a perilous realm of swords and monsters, this expertise is a lifeline, sustaining adventurers through brutal battles or cursed afflictions. It’s the difference between life and death, wielded by clerics, medics, and alchemists alike.

##### Tracking (*skill*)

This expertise allows characters to follow trails left by creatures or individuals, identifying footprints, broken branches, and other signs of passage. Skilled trackers can determine the number of beings, their size, and even their speed. This is invaluable for hunting monsters, pursuing fugitives, or avoiding ambushes in the wilderness. In a world of magic and adventure, tracking might also reveal traces of enchanted beasts or hidden lairs.

##### Crafting (*skill*)

Characters with this expertise can create or repair a variety of items, from simple tools to complex magical artifacts. This includes forging swords, brewing potions, or enchanting armor to withstand monster attacks. The quality and effectiveness of the crafted items depend on the character's level of expertise. Such skills are essential for adventurers needing custom gear to face the perils of a fantasy realm.

## Agility

##### Jumping (*agility*)

Jumping is the physical skill of vaulting over obstacles, spanning gaps, or reaching great heights. It’s crucial for crossing treacherous landscapes, fleeing monstrous foes, or accessing hidden perches in ancient ruins. In a fantasy world, magical boots or spells might boost leaps to defy gravity itself. Adventurers rely on this ability to navigate perilous realms with agility and daring.

##### Balancing (*agility*)

Balancing is the knack for staying steady on narrow or shaky surfaces, like tightropes, crumbling ledges, or storm-swept ship decks. It demands sharp agility and focus to avoid falls or resist being toppled by foes. In battle or hazardous terrains—think icy caves or enchanted spans—this skill keeps characters upright and in control. It’s a must for those who tread the most unstable paths.

##### Climbing (*agility*)

Climbing is the expertise of scaling sheer surfaces, from rugged cliffs to the walls of forgotten castles. Experts use ropes, grappling hooks, or even magic like spider climb spells to ascend with confidence. This skill unlocks high treasures, escapes from danger, or vantage points for combat. In a world of towering peaks and lost ruins, climbing paves the way to adventure.

##### Running (*agility*)

Running is the ability to dash quickly on foot, whether pursuing enemies, escaping threats, or racing across vast expanses. It’s key for closing in on foes, outrunning monsters, or covering ground in a hurry. In a fantasy realm, enchanted boots or speed potions might push runners beyond natural limits. This expertise fuels adventurers who thrive on speed and urgency.

##### Stealth (*agility*)

Stealth is the art of moving unseen and unheard, merging with shadows to evade detection. It includes hiding, silent movement, and using surroundings to stay concealed. Magic like invisibility spells or light-bending cloaks can amplify this skill in a fantastical world. It’s indispensable for thieves, scouts, and anyone infiltrating lairs or dodging deadly guardians.

##### Sailing (*agility*)

Sailing is the expertise of commanding watercraft, from tiny boats to majestic ships, across open seas. It requires understanding tides, weather, and sail mechanics to navigate safely. In a fantasy setting, sailors face magical storms, sea beasts, or cursed waters with courage and skill. This ability drives ocean-bound quests, island hunts, and epic naval clashes.

##### Escape Artist (*agility*)

Escape Artist is the expertise of wriggling free from physical restraints—such as ropes, chains, or magical bindings—or squeezing through confined spaces like narrow crevices or barred windows. It relies on exceptional flexibility, precise body control, and quick thinking to contort and maneuver out of entrapment. In a world filled with dungeons, traps, and monstrous captors, this skill is essential for adventurers who find themselves bound, imprisoned, or cornered. Masters of Escape Artist can slip out of combat grapples, break free from captivity, or bypass seemingly impassable obstacles, transforming vulnerability into opportunity with agile cunning.

##### Swimming (*agility*)

Proficiency in swimming allows characters to move efficiently through water, whether crossing rivers, exploring underwater caves, or escaping aquatic monsters. It includes techniques for holding breath, diving, and rescuing others from drowning. This skill is vital for adventures involving sunken treasures or battles against sea-dwelling creatures in a fantasy setting.

## Aim

##### Throwing (*aim*)

This expertise measures your proficiency in accurately propelling objects through the air, whether for combat or practical purposes. Throwing also proves valuable in adventurous tasks requiring accurate projection, such as tossing a rope to an ally across a chasm, hurling a grappling hook to scale a crumbling ruin, or even lobbing a torch into a shadowy lair to reveal hidden dangers. A high Throwing expertise reflects exceptional hand-eye coordination and the ability to judge distances and trajectories effectively, making it a versatile skill.

##### Construction (*aim*)

Construction is the expertise of erecting structures, from basic shelters to formidable stone fortresses. Masters of this craft understand materials, engineering, and tool use to build sturdy defenses or breach enemy walls. In a magical world, construction might weave in enchanted stones for resilience or runes to repel mystical threats. This skill is essential for adventurers seeking to create safe refuges or dismantle obstacles in their path.

##### Artistry (*aim*)

Artistry covers the creation of visual and performing arts, like painting, sculpture, music, and dance. In a magical setting, it can entertain, inspire, or even weave spells through captivating performances. Skilled artists might conjure illusions, sway emotions, or encode secrets in their creations. This expertise shines for bards, enchanters, and heroes who wield creativity to overcome challenges or rally allies.

##### Fletching (*aim*)

The art of making arrows and bolts, ensuring they are balanced and aerodynamic for accurate flight. Expert fletchers can create specialized ammunition, such as fire arrows or poison-tipped bolts, enhancing their effectiveness in combat against monsters. This skill supports archers and crossbow users by providing tailored projectiles for specific threats.

##### Navigation (*aim*)

Using maps, compasses, and celestial bodies, characters with this expertise can determine their location and plot courses through unknown territories. It is vital for sea voyages, desert treks, or forest expeditions where getting lost could mean encountering deadly creatures. In a magical world, navigation might also involve reading enchanted star charts or avoiding cursed lands.

##### Artillery Operation (*aim*)

This expertise involves the use of siege weapons like catapults, ballistae, and trebuchets to target massive monsters or enemy strongholds. Characters skilled in artillery can accurately aim these machines to breach walls, sink ships, or decimate enemy formations from afar. It’s a powerful asset for large-scale battles or defending against giant beasts.

##### Gem Cutting (*aim*)

Gem Cutting expertise represents a character’s mastery in shaping and polishing gems with meticulous accuracy, enhancing their beauty, value, or magical potency. Proficient gem cutters can transform raw stones into dazzling jewels, cut facets to capture light perfectly, or even inscribe tiny runes to imbue the gems with enchantments. In a realm where gems often hold mystical significance—powering artifacts, focusing spells, or serving as currency—this skill is crucial for crafters, enchanters, and treasure hunters alike. It demands a steady hand, keen eye, and deep understanding of crystalline structures, allowing characters to unlock the full potential of these precious resources while showcasing their finesse and attention to detail.

##### Calligraphy (*aim*)

Calligraphy is the art of elegant and precise writing, a skill that blends artistic flair with meticulous control, making it a prized expertise in a world where words can hold both beauty and power. Characters proficient in Calligraphy can create stunning manuscripts, forge official documents with uncanny accuracy, or inscribe runes and symbols that enhance the potency of magical scrolls and artifacts. In a realm where the written word can unlock ancient secrets or bind powerful spells, this expertise is invaluable for scholars, enchanters, and deceivers alike. The steady hand and keen eye required for Calligraphy reflect a character’s exceptional precision, allowing them to craft works of art or magical inscriptions with flawless accuracy, whether they are illuminating a royal decree or etching a rune onto a blade.

Books and scrolls have been your battlefield, filled with lessons from history and theory. You analyze problems with a calm, methodical mind, finding solutions others overlook. Your broad knowledge lets you predict outcomes and devise clever plans. Wisdom, not muscle, is your strength.

##### Scribe

Copying ancient texts and deciphering faded runes has steeped you in magical lore. Your quill dances across parchment, recording spells and secrets few understand. Gathering roots and leaves from wild places has taught you the magic woven into the earth. You brew teas that soothe and salves that mend, each tied to old traditions. Your knowledge of plants borders on the mystical, a quiet power born of patience. The forest whispers its secrets to you.

Surrounded by dusty tomes, you’ve unearthed fragments of arcane truth amid endless pages. You catalog and study, piecing together spells and histories lost to time. Your mind is a repository of knowledge, sharp and attuned to magic’s echoes. Silence and ink are your gateways to power.

A life of prayer and asceticism has tempered your spirit and body into unyielding steel. You’ve fasted, meditated, and faced inner demons in silent cloisters. Physical hardship and mental discipline are your daily bread, forging a will few can match. Serenity hides your stubborn strength.

In *Heroic Adventures*, every creature is a member of a certain race, such as human, elf, dwarf, goblin, demon, or ogre. Character races, or the races that you can play as a player, are limited to the ones listed in this chapter, and shown in character creation.

Each character race will have certain starting stat modifiers to the primary stats of skill, agility, intelligence, willpower, strength, and initiative. These can be modified during character creation and level advancement. Equipment will also modify the effective statistics that you will use through the course of gameplay.

Depending upon the setting that you are playing in, which is determined by the game master, each race might have different allegiances, feelings, and grudges against other races. It is common for elves and dwarves to not get along, for example, but for halflings to get along with everyone.

<img src="assets/images/media/image13.png" style="width:2.11736in;height:3.51319in" />In addition to your affecting your primary stats, your race will also give you a certain amount of health during character creation and each time you level up.

Your race will also give you features that you can acquire as you level up. These features can give you heroic actions or adjust other aspects of your character.

Some races are tough, like dwarves and half orcs. Other races are quick on their feet, like halflings and elves. Others are balanced, such as humans. Choosing your race is one of the most defining choices you make as you create your character.

Your race will also affect how you roleplay. Some towns or cities might not take kindly to dwarves, for example. This might be due to a war that was fought with the dwarves that is still in the living memory of some of the elders of the community. Or alternatively, elves might be distrusted due to their elusive behavior.

Depending on the world created by the game master and the players, your choice of race will affect gameplay in combat and roleplay out of combat.

An important factor that comes into play during combat is your major and minor movement speed. Major movement speed is how far you can move before or after an attack. Minor movement speed is how far you can move between your major actions. This minor movement speed allows you to get out of attack range at the right time, position yourself in the right place as combat unfolds, and more. Your selection of race will largely determine both attributes.

## About Halflings

Halflings do not keep records of their history, so what is known about them is largely because of the humans who have welcomed them into communities and kingdoms. Halflings seem to be everywhere, with consistent habits and customs. This includes their propensity to till the land, dwell in peace with civilized races, provide wares and services in markets, and operate inns as havens for travelers.

Wherever there are human villages and cities, halflings commonly build smaller settlements nearby. This is a way to live within the protection of humans, who offer safety in exchange for the benefits halflings provide to human communities. This includes sustenance, local knowledge of the land, and artisanal items and equipment.

Some halflings feel the call to adventure, finding joy in exploring the natural beauty of wild lands. When faced with combat, they are elusive and often take advantage of stealth, surprise, and exploiting weaknesses.

Other halflings develop a deep connection to the land that they dwell in, allowing them to draw upon its natural powers as a font of magic. These halflings are often the community sage, providing wisdom and experience to younger members.

As they increase their connection to the powers of nature, they can become more withdrawn from society. These halflings become an important source of knowledge for anyone seeking to understand the land and its animal inhabitants. However, this knowledge is not given easily and often must be earned by caring for and protecting the land, including the animals and people that dwell on it.

Classes

In *Heroic Adventures*, every player character will have a character class. Each character class will give you certain benefits during character creation and level advancement. The most significant of these benefits are features, which you will select at certain levels as described in the level advancement section.

There are 4 basic classes: fighter, wizard, rogue, and cleric. These are the standard roles that a character can take when adventuring through a fantastical world.

The *fighter* is someone who takes up weapons and armor and leads his allies into the unknown. A fighter will take on challenges that others might back away from and is used to being in the chaos of combat. It is recommended that players new to tabletop roleplaying play one of the basic classes.

The *wizard* is someone who casts spells to overcome enemies. Often wizards are not strong in combat, although over time they can gain the ability to be a combat wizard or can specialize in powerful spellcasting.

The *rogue* likes to stay out of the way of the enemy and take advantage of their weaknesses.

The *cleric* is a warrior who can also cast spells, mostly focused on healing and helping their friends. Clerics are also capable of powerfully confronting undead creatures.

There are also advanced classes. These classes have more complicated abilities and usually change the standard rules of play in some way. It is recommended to only play the advanced classes if you are an experienced tabletop role player.

The *priest* is devoted to a religious order and uses divine intervention to aid his companions in combat.

The *paladin* is a divine warrior who, like the fighter, wants to be in the midst of combat. A paladin is unafraid of combat and even death itself.

The *warlock* is a ruthless spell caster who uses his own lifeblood to cast devastating incantations.

The *barbarian* is a warrior who focuses on aggression and power to overcome his enemies.

The *druid* uses the forces of nature to cast powerful spells. However, this primordial magic can be dangerous and can hurt friends and foes alike.

The *ranger* is a wilderness warrior trained in the skills of the wild.

# Wizard

## Character creation

+3 health

Reduce your strength and skill by 1. You cannot choose increases to strength or skill during the Apply Stat Increases step of character creation.

Acquire 2 mystic spells

Your mana equals your intelligence + your character level

In order to cast spells, a wizard must wield a staff or a wand. If you choose a staff, you can only cast spells while wielding a staff and you gain +2 mana. If you choose a wand, you can only cast spells while using a wand and you gain +1 intelligence. A wand provides +4 initiative when equipped as a primary weapon and requires that nothing is equipped in your off hand in order to cast spells. Gain a wand or staff for free.

You can modify the result of a mage’s guild roll up or down by 1.

You may wield a staff or wand one handed as a secondary weapon and cast spells even when having another weapon equipped as a primary weapon. Do not draw opportunity attacks when preparing and casting spells.

### Warrior Casting: Feature

Cast spells even while you are not equipped with a staff or wand.

### Armored Casting: Feature

Cast spells even while wearing medium armor.

### Greater Armored Casting: Feature

### Spell practice: Feature

Acquire 2 mystic spells.

### Source: Feature

Gain 3 mana.

### Greater Source: Feature

Gain 3 mana. Requires **Source**.

### Guild Apprentice: Feature

Acquire 1 mystic spell. You may spend 1 hour to make a roll on the mages guild table without being at the mage’s guild.

### Archmage: Feature

Acquire 1 mystic spell. You may modify the result of a mage’s guild roll up or down by 3. Requires **Scholar** and intelligence 5+.

+3 health

Gain expertise in two action checks

Gain +2 for each expertise level instead of +1

You cannot choose increases to strength or willpower during the Apply Stat Increases step of character creation.

## Advancement

+2 health

Gain expertise in two action checks.

## Features

### Back stab: Heroic Action

Make an attack against a creature you are flanking and gain an extra weapon damage dice.

### Quick Strike: Heroic Action

Make an initiative vs. initiative attack.

### Set Trap: Heroic Action

Place a marker in a space up to 3 spaces away and choose one of the below effects. If any creature moves into or out of the space with the marker or an adjacent space, make a skill vs. agility roll and remove the marker. If the attack hits, apply the chosen effect. You may use this ability outside of combat without counting the heroic action as being used.

**Poison dart**: 1d6 damage that ignores armor at the start of each round. If you roll a 5 or 6 for damage, the effect ends.

**Sticky slime**: The target cannot make major moves until they spend a major action and succeed on a strength vs. skill check against you.

**Arrow**: The target takes 2d6 damage.

### Two weapon fighting: Minor Action

Attack with your secondary weapon. In order to use this feature, you must have two weapons equipped and both must be small.

### Twin weapon fighting: Minor Action

Attack with your secondary weapon. In order to use this feature, you must have two weapons equipped and your off hand weapon must be small. Requires Two weapon fighting.

### Weak Spot: Minor Action

If you are flanking an enemy creature, you may place a single d4 on this action. The number of d4s that can be on this feature at the same time is equal to your heroic actions per day limit. The next back stab heroic action you take against a creature, you may spend these d4 to add to your skill roll or damage roll. You can only use this ability against one creature at a time. Requires: Back stab

### Trap Expert: Feature

Increase the damage of your traps by one damage dice and place two traps each time you take the action. Requires skill 5+.

### Exploit: Feature

Gain +3 with advantage instead of +2

### Dualist: Feature

Gain advantage with agility against melee attacks when neither you nor the attacker are adjacent to an ally

Spells

Spells are powerful abilities that often provide attacks that target attributes other than agility. They are acquired through advancement and are provided by your race or class. Spells are versatile, allowing the spell caster to look for the weaknesses of any particular creature and exploit them. Additionally, they can provide area effect, control over enemies, or even the initiative order, and can provide healing and other benefits to allies.

##### Casting spells

Casting minor spells requires using a minor action, and casting major spells requires using a major action. You need direct line of sight to the target of the spell, whether that is a space or creature. A major action can also be used to cast a minor spell.

When casting a spell, you may spend mana to add enhancements to the spell, spending one mana for each enhancement. At level 1, up to 1 enhancement can be added. The maximum number of enhancements that can be added increases at even levels. The same enhancement can be added multiple times. A character regains all of their mana after a long rest.

When casting a major spell, mana is not spent immediately and the spell does not occur immediately, but the character begins preparing the spell. The round continues until the “all players” card is drawn from the initiative deck (or the “all monsters” card is drawn, if a monster is casting a major spell). At this point, all characters (or all monsters) cast their prepared spell. At this point mana is spent and the spell takes place. If a creature is attacked while they have a spell prepared, they suffer disadvantage to their defense roll. If they take damage while a spell is prepared, the player must roll 1d6 damage against themselves that ignores damage reduction.

Preparing and casting spells draws opportunity attacks from creatures within melee range. Unless otherwise specified, spells cannot be cast while wearing medium or heavy armor or while using a shield.

Any spell that lasts for 1 round, lasts until the end of the current round. Any spell that lasts for 2 rounds, lasts until the end of the next round, and so on.

Equipment

When not adventuring, characters can spend their hard-earned coins on equipment such as higher tier weapons and armor, torches, food and lodging. The mages guild can turn unidentified into one single use spell scrolls. The more unidentified scrolls that are used on a single roll the more powerful the spell. This can be risky though, because half of all rolls result in no spell scroll. The alchemist’s guild uses an alternative currency of ether with which potions can be purchased. This means that acquiring potions and scrolls does not prevent players from saving up for high tier equipment. The alchemist’s guild can even swap the spell enchantments of items.

# <img src="assets/images/media/image38.png" style="width:8.98542in;height:4.49236in" />Armor

In *Heroic Adventures*, armor is critical to staying alive. While it slows you down, as represented by the decrease in agility, it also gives you toughness, which is subtracted from most damage that you take. While it might seem like the penalty for wearing armor is high, in reality that increased toughness means that you are preserving your health every time that you get hit, and this is a massive benefit.

In rare cases it might make sense to maximize your agility so that you can avoid getting hit, and risk that when you get hit you will take a lot of damage. This can be done effectively, but it is risky, and it means that one bad role could be very dangerous for your character. Normally, going into battle without armor is a bad idea. A rogue that likes to sneak around and avoid being targeted might be a character that benefits from not wearing armor.

In some cases, however, armor can be less than helpful. If you are fighting a giant that is swinging a tree trunk as a weapon, your toughness might not be helpful. Subtracting 6 damage from a massive attack that does 4d6 damage is not going to help. If the giant hits you, no amount of armor can save you. In this case, it might be worth shedding the armor and avoid getting hit at all costs.

In other circumstances, such as when you are fighting against small weapons from many opponents, armor can make you nearly invincible to their attacks. Every hit they do might barely do enough damage, if any, to penetrate your damage reduction. When this happens, your opponents are going to have to look for ways to get around your armor. This can be done with attacks that ignore armor such as fire attacks, mental attacks, spells, and so forth.

# Shields

In *Heroic Adventures*, shields are the first line of defense. When rolling a defense roll, a shield provides a static chance that you block the opponent’s attack, regardless of the skill of the attacker. Best of all, this happens when you roll low on your agility roll, meaning that when you are most likely to be hit by an attack, you block the attack instead. See the combat section in the gameplay chapter for more information regarding attack and defense rolls.

Each shield will have a size of small, medium, or large, which could be referenced by various game effects. In addition, shields modify your initiative, meaning that you are slightly less quick on your feet than you would otherwise be. However, this comes with the benefit of providing a block value, meaning that when you roll equal to or lower than the listed block value, the attack misses completely. Block values are only used when you are rolling an agility defense roll.

Some attacks might mention that they ignore shields, in which case you would not get this benefit. Examples of these kinds of attacks are area effects, some heroic actions, and large attacks such as a stomp of a giant. In these circumstances, especially the last example, a shield is clearly not going to help!

# Healing House

As explained in the rules chapter, after reaching zero health a character is considered to be dying. The healing house is a place for such characters. A dying character cannot move or take actions on their own, so someone else would need to bring the character to the healing house. Assuming that the dying character was brought to the healing house in time, coin can be spent trying to revive that character and bring them back to stable condition.

After spending coin, roll a d20 and add your willpower, plus the healing benefit received from spending the coin. If the total is a 20 or more, the character spends 7 days recovering. On the 12th day they are returned to 1 health. Healing potions, spells, and long rests are not able to return health to the character while they are recovering. If the character fails this d20 check, they die as the attendants of the healing house attempt to revive them.

A character that is recovering can talk and move around their room at a slow pace but cannot move faster than one space per turn and cannot make attacks.

Proteges

As you adventure, the chance of a character dying increases. Death could come from anywhere; dragons, armies, undead lords, or a simple goblin spear that happens to be at the right place at the wrong time. When a character dies, you will of course need to create a new character. However, if you start from level 1 and everyone else is at higher level, then you will find it hard to contribute to the adventure. Protégé’s are designed to solve this problem.

Every great hero, and not so great hero, has someone that he has taken under his wing. Someone to confide in, someone he can trust. This other character learns and grows along with the hero and is always looking for his moment to shine. A time to step up and take his place in the world.

It's just unfortunate that the bad luck of the hero is the long-awaited moment to shine of the protégé, but sometimes that is how it goes.

When you reach level 3 you may create a new character to be your protégé. Your protégé increases in level any time your primary character increases in level. If your primary character dies, you may play your protégé character with all the same in-world knowledge of your primary character. It is recommended to work with the game master to determine your primary character’s relationship to your protégé, why they know each other, and why they share information with each other.

If your protégé becomes your primary character and he is level 3, you may create a new protégé. If your new primary character is higher than level 3, make a protégé character and increase their level such that they are two levels lower than your new primary character.

In this way, you will always have a character waiting in the wing. From a game mechanics perspective this keeps everyone at similar level. It also grows the anticipation to try out a new character, and a character that can quickly be introduced in the middle of a gaming session.

From a roleplay perspective, protégé’s provide an opportunity to expand the game world and provide the main characters relationships with other characters. Where do the characters go when they are not adventuring, who do they spend time with, and where do they live? The protégé characters might be involved in answering all of these questions.

Also, if you want a break from the main story, everyone can play their protégé character for a session. This can be a side quest to get the players used to their new characters and offer a change up to the normal game session.

Mass Combat

At higher levels, characters could fight hordes or armies of enemies. The game master can group creatures that are significantly lower level than the characters into units. When creating a unit, use the statistics of the creature as normal except modify the statistics as shown on the below table.

### Unit Sizes

| Unit size | \# of creatures | Modify statistics | Damage dice |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Warband   | 5               | +2                | +1          |
| Host      | 10              | +3                | +2          |

### Unit Stats

All base statistics, including skill, agility, intelligence, willpower, strength, and initiative are modified by the value from the above table. A units health characteristic is equal to the normal health characteristic of that creature.

### Unit Health Modifications

For creatures that have 12 or more health, it is recommended to reduce the health characteristic by 1 or more and add 1 or more additional creatures to the unit to make up for the lost health. For example, if a lizardman has 16 health, then a warband of 5 lizardmen could have 13 health. This would mean that a total of 15 health was lost by this reduction. To make up for this lost health, one additional lizardmen could be added to the unit, to make up 13 of the 15 lost health. This keeps the total health of the almost the same, but allows for the unit to more easily sustain casualties. It is recommend to make this health modification for any creatures that have 15 or more health, as an attack has to do that much damage just to kill a single creature from the unit.

### Moving Units

A unit that charges into contact with an enemy unit must move straight towards that unit, only turning to avoid obstacles. They must engage that unit on the side that they were facing when they began the charge. Unit formation should be roughly square or rectangular with a clear front line. Any side of the unit that is not the front line is considered the flank.

Once engaged in combat, units cannot make minor movements. When being charged by another unit or an individual character, a unit can turn to face the attacker, if that unit is not already engaged. Individual characters making minor movements while in combat range of a unit must use their minor movement to move straight away from that unit.

### Attacking Units

Units make only a single attack when attacking and roll a single defense roll when being attacked. When being attacked from a side of the unit that is not the front line, the unit gets disadvantage to its agility defense roll. If the unit is not already engaged in melee, then when being charged, a unit can turn to face the charging creature or unit in order to ensure that they charge the front line of the unit.

### Unit Casualties

When taking damage, units track casualties instead of health. Damage reduction is applied as normal. Any attack that does damage equal to or greater than the unit’s health characteristic causes the unit to take a casualty. If an attack doubles the unit’s health characteristic, the unit takes two casualties, and so forth. The health characteristic of a unit is equal to the normal health characteristic for the creature.

### Bravery Checks

After taking casualties, if over half the creatures in a unit have been killed, that unit must make a bravery check. Make a willpower vs. willpower roll against the attacker. If the unit loses this check, they flee the battlefield, making an immediate full move away from the attacker, causing opportunity attacks. Fleeing units continue to flee the battlefield each time their initiative comes up, unless they are rallied.

### Rally

Any character or individual leader amongst an army may attempt to rally a fleeing unit by moving adjacent to the unit and spending their major action rallying the unit. A character adjacent to a unit that is about to make a bravery check can spend his major action in order to give that unit advantage to their rally check.

### Area of Effect

Any attacks that do area of affect damage hit units as a single attack roll. Gain one damage dice for each model within the unit that is covered by the area of affect.

### Army Composition

These mass combat rules can be used to allow a group of characters to fight a dozen or two goblins but can also be used to have two armies clash in large scale combat of up to a hundred or more creatures.

It is recommended to have one individual creature act as a leader, such as an orc chief, for every three units. The player characters can act as leaders for one side of the battle and can control the movement of the units on that side. The players could decide who amongst them will control which units, while the game master controls all the units and creatures on the other side of the battlefield. It can also be fun to mix in ranged units, melee units, individual leader creatures, and individual monsters such as ogres, trolls, and giants.

Units can also be given additional features that cause them to play differently than other units. The same unit can have multiple features.

Gameplay

# Introduction

In Heroic Adventures, characters take different types of actions depending on the encounter deck. Action types are minor, major, heroic, bonus, and opportunity actions as explained in the combat section below.

## Features

Features can be one-time benefits to statistics, ongoing effects, reactions that can be used as described by the action, or actions that can be taken at various times depending upon the type of action.

When gaining a feature, write down the feature on your character sheet in one of the squares provided on the character sheet. As shown on the character sheet, you may have up to five heroic actions, listed on the bottom left of the character sheet, and five features of other types, listed on the bottom right of the character sheet.

You may not select a class or race feature that would give you more than five heroic actions. Additionally, you may not select a class or race feature that would give you five features of other types. This includes static bonuses, reactions, major actions, minor actions, bonus actions, and opportunity actions.

# Combat

Combat begins when one or more creature’s feel threatened by or begins attacking another group of creatures. Combat is started at the discretion of the game master.

## Starting the combat round

Determine the monster level range for the encounter. If an encounter has monsters of varying levels, the game master uses his best judgement to determine the monster level range to use. A party of characters could have characters of varying levels and could be fighting weaker monsters or may be fighting monsters of higher level in a difficult encounter. Once the monster level range is determined, take the 10 initiative cards for that level range. These 10 cards form the encounter deck.

Five of these cards will be blue, indicating that the players can take actions on those cards. Four of these blue cards will indicate a range of initiatives. Players with those initiatives can take a major action on that card, and possibly a bonus action if it was the first card flipped or if the initiative range on that card is higher than the previous card. One of the blue cards will allow all players to take either a minor action or a heroic action.

Five of the cards in the encounter deck will be red. Four of these cards will have initiative ranges indicating that monsters with those initiatives can take a major action and possibly a bonus action. One of the red cards will allow all monsters to take a minor action or a heroic action.

If multiple enemy creatures have the same initiative, then they activate all on the same turn, one by one. If multiple player characters have the same initiative, then they take actions in clockwise order, or any order the player’s prefer.

To begin an encounter, shuffle the encounter deck and then place all miniatures representing characters and monsters on a battle map with appropriate terrain or markings for the landscape that is being fought on.

## Round sequence

To begin the round, shuffle the encounter deck. Now, flip a card from the encounter deck. The creatures with the shown initiative (the number in the middle of the card) may now take either a minor, major, or heroic action. Creatures can only take one major action per round.

### Surprise

In some circumstances, one or more creatures in the encounter might be surprised. They may have been ambushed along the road, attacked while eating a meal, or otherwise were not expecting a fight. In this case, any surprised creature skips their first initiative card on the first round.

### Heroic actions

During a heroic action, a creature can make a major movement. After using a heroic action, the number of remaining heroic actions indicated on your character sheet should be decreased. Your remaining heroic actions resets after a long rest.

Bravery potions allow characters to use heroic actions without decreasing your remaining heroic actions count. Some heroic actions will indicate other restrictions on when and how they can be used.

### Opportunity actions

If a threatened creature uses a major movement to leave a space that is within melee range, and the space they are moving into is the same distance away from you or further, you may take an opportunity action, even if it is not your turn. The most common opportunity action is an opportunity attack, which you can see below in the standard actions section. Minor movements do not draw opportunity actions. Any major movement that only moves a single space does not draw opportunity actions.

### Bonus actions

If the currently flipped card’s number range is greater than the previous card’s number range, then the activating creatures may take a bonus action. If the previous flipped card did not have a number range, then the creatures activating on the current card do not get a bonus action. If one of the 8 cards with a number range is flipped as the first card of the round, those creatures get a bonus action.

GAMEPLAY INSIGHT: The lowest initiative range has a 10% chance of getting a bonus action each round. The next initiative range has a 30% chance of a getting a bonus action. The next initiative range has a 50% chance of getting a bonus action. The highest initiative range has a 70% chance of getting a bonus action.

### Reactions

Some features are considered reactions. These actions can be taken when specified by the feature. However, each creature can only take one reaction per initiative card.

### End of round

After every card has been drawn from the encounter deck the combat round ends. If the combat is not over, start a new combat round. Continue combat rounds until the combat ends.

#### Threatened

Any creature that is within melee range of an enemy is considered to be threatened.

# Actions

When a creature’s card is flipped from the encounter deck, that creature may take a major action. All humanoid creatures can take the standard actions shown in the Standard Actions section below.

## Attack actions

When a creature makes an attack the attacker and defender both roll a d20.

#### Shields

If the defender is using a shield and the face value of the dice is equal to or less than their block, the attack misses. Shields are the most common way to get block but can only be used against attacks that target agility. Other effects such as spells and features can provide block as well. Some attacks will say they ignore shields. For these attacks you do not get to use your block value from shields. Although, if you have gained a block value from another effect, such as a spell or cover, you would still get to use that block value.

#### Adding modifiers

The attacker adds the indicated stat to the roll. If no stat is specified, weapon attacks use skill and spell attacks use intelligence. The defender adds their indicated stat to their roll. If not specified, weapon attacks target the defender’s agility and spell attacks target the defender’s willpower. If the attacker’s modified roll exceeded the defenders modified roll, then the attack hits. Otherwise, the attack misses.

#### Weapon training

Weapon training increases initiative for each point of training you have in that weapon category, when using weapons within that category.

#### Advantage

Some situations and features will provide advantage to a dice roll. Any dice roll with advantage gains a +2 modifier in addition to any other modifiers you may have. If you have advantage from multiple sources, the modifier is still only +2.

#### Damage

If the attack hits, roll the damage dice for the attack and add together the result of all the dice. Melee weapon attacks add the attacker’s strength to the damage total. Then, subtract the defender’s toughness from the damage total. This is the total damage the target takes. Subtract the total damage from the defender’s current health. Some attacks will specify that they ignore toughness, in which case the defender’s toughness is not subtracted from the damage. Other attacks will specify that they ignore armor, in which case the defender’s armor is not included in their toughness. With these attacks, toughness gained from other effects, such as spells or features, would still apply.

#### Exploding dice

Damage dice explode. This means that when rolling the maximum result on the dice you roll that dice again and add the next roll to the total. You keep rerolling until you do not roll the maximum result of that dice.

### Temporary Health

Some effects will provide temporary health. When taking damage, subtract damage from your temporary health before subtracting damage from your current health. If you gain temporary health while already having temporary health, do not add the temporary health together. Instead, take whichever is higher, your current temporary health, or the new temporary health. Remove any temporary health during a long rest.

## Ranged Attacks

Ranged attacks are performed just like melee attacks except that they draw opportunity actions from adjacent creatures and are aim vs. tactics apposed rolls. Most commonly this means that if you make a ranged attack while an enemy is adjacent to you, they may make an opportunity attack against you. You may only shoot through friendly creatures if that friendly creature is adjacent to you.

If you are making a ranged attack against a creature and miss, all creatures, including allies, adjacent to the original target must make an agility roll. Compare the creature who rolled the lowest agility to your original attack roll. If the agility roll was lower than your attack roll, then they are hit by your attack, regardless of whether they were an ally or an enemy.

## Cover

At the game master’s discretion, a character can gain cover from their environment against ranged attacks. When using cover, compare your unmodified agility roll to the cover value. If your unmodified agility roll is equal to or less than the cover value, then the attack misses. Some attacks will ignore certain types of cover. For example, a bush will not protect you from a hill giant swinging a tree trunk, but it might protect you from a club.

## Opportunity actions

During a major action, if a creature makes a major movement and leaves a space that is adjacent to another creature, that other creature may take an opportunity action. The creature that moved as to provide you the opportunity action is called the “target”. Some opportunity actions will mention the “target” of the opportunity. Some actions will say that they “give opportunity” in which case adjacent enemy creatures may take an opportunity action.

## Dual Wielding

You may wield two one handed weapons simultaneously. If doing so, the larger weapon is considered your primary weapon and the smaller weapon is considered your secondary weapon. If both weapons are the same size, you must declare which one is your primary weapon and which one is your secondary weapon. When attacking, you may only attack with your primary weapon unless an action specifies otherwise. You only get the initiative bonus from your primary weapon. Race, class, and item features can provide benefits to dual wielding.

## Standard actions

Most creatures have some standard actions that are always available. Some non-humanoid monsters might not have access to some of these actions, such as a wolf cannot use the throw action.

### Minor Move: Minor action

Move a number of spaces equal to your minor movement speed. This movement does *not* draw opportunity actions.

### Major Move: Major action

Move a number of spaces equal to your major movement speed. This movement draws opportunity actions.

### Draw or Change Weapons: Major action

You may swap your active weapon profile. Your new initiative is taken into consideration at the start of the next round. You cannot take this action if you have already taken a bonus action this round. If you take this action, you cannot take bonus actions for the rest of the round.

### All Out Defense: Reaction

Before making an agility defense roll you may take this reaction as long as you have not taken a major action this turn. Gain advantage on the agility roll. You may not take a major action for the remainder of the turn.

### Attack: Major action

Make a melee or ranged attack. You may move a number of spaces equal to your major movement speed before or after the attack. You may also split your movement and move some spaces before the attack and some spaces after the attack, but you may only move a total number of spaces equal to your major movement speed. This movement draws opportunity actions.

### Heroic Attack: Heroic action

Take the attack major action.

### Charge: Major action

Move at least 3 spaces in a straight line directly towards a target. Make an attack against that target.

### Unarmed Melee Attack: Major Action

Make a melee attack. Damage is equal to your strength with no dice roll.

### Unarmed Ranged Attack: Major Action

Make a ranged attack up to 6 spaces away that does 1d4 damage with no modifiers. This requires a heavy throwable object to be in your hand or nearby.

### Throw: Minor Action

Throw an object up to a number of spaces equal to 3 + your strength. Make a throwing action check against a difficulty of 10. If you make the check, the object lands on the target. If you fail the check, the object lands on a random space adjacent to the target.

### Push: Minor action

Make an apposed strength check against a target within melee range. If successful, move the target 5 ft in any direction. The target falls prone if your strength check doubled their strength check. This action can only be taken once per round.

### Grapple: Minor action

Make either an apposed strength check, or an apposed skill check against a target within melee range. If successful, you and the target are grappled for the remainder of the round. Grappled targets cannot take move actions.

### Opportunity Attack: Opportunity action

Make a melee attack against the target.

### Bonus Attack: Bonus action

Make a melee attack against the target. You may move before and after the attack as normal.

## Long Rest

A long rest is 8 hours of continuous rest without interruption by hostile creatures. During this time characters must spend 6 hours sleeping. After a long rest, the character regains one health per level. In addition, they regain all their mana and access to their heroic actions. If you rested at an inn or other indoor comfortable location with food and water, double the amount of healing you receive.

## Consumables

You can keep at most 4 types of consumables available for use at any time, with a maximum quantity of 4 each. You can carry more (in your rucksack or inventory) but you cannot have more than 4 types of consumables ready to be accessed and used in combat at any one time. Each different type of potion and scroll is considered a different type of consumable and requires its own slot on your character sheet.

Most consumables are used with a minor action. When not in combat, you may refill your consumables slots from your inventory in a few minutes.

When you gain a consumable, write the name of the consumable in one of the four slots on the front of your character sheet in the consumables section along with the quantity. Any consumables that cannot be fit into this ready access section can be noted down in your inventory on the back of your character sheet.

When you take a minor action to use a consumable, reduce the quantity of the consumable in the ready access section. Some consumables can be used as a reaction. In either case, a single character can use at most one consumable per action. Consumables include potions, scrolls, and other single use objects.

## Dying

When a character takes damage that takes them to zero health or less but are *not* reduced to negative 10 health or fewer, then they are considered to be dying. Any other character with at least one expertise in healing can try to keep the dying character alive by spending 5 minutes applying bandages. That character can make a healing action check. If the result is 8 or greater, then that result is the number of hours that the dying character remains alive. Otherwise, the character dies.

A dying character cannot move or take actions by themselves. Healing potions, spells, and long rests do not give health to a dying character.

The main way to keep them alive is to take them to a healing house. A character with three selections of the healing skill may attempt to revive a dying character per the healing house rules, with a +1 bonus. A character with 4 selections of the healing skill gains +2 to the revive roll, and a character with 5 selections of the healing skill gains a +3 bonus. This revival check must be made in comfortable conditions such as a home or an inn and requires spending 100 coin on various supplies needed such as bandages and medical equipment.

Game Master’s Manual

One player will take on the title of game master. The game master’s primary responsibility is to create the setting, populate it with creatures and non-playable characters, and present this world to the players. The game master will play as all the creatures in the world that are not the player characters and will arbitrate the rules if there are any questions. It is suggested that the most experienced player is the game master.

Heroic Adventures is designed to be an easy to play pen and paper roleplaying game. While experienced game masters will have their tried-and-true methods, they along with inexperienced game masters might be looking for advice when it comes to this specific game. This chapter will go over some details of game mastering that are specific to Heroic Adventures and then share some tips for running a pen and paper roleplaying game.

It is also important to remember that everything in this chapter is a suggestion and should be modified as you see fit. Use your own judgement when creating encounters that you think will be easy or difficult, when determining how much treasure to give, and so forth. You are encouraged to come up with your own monsters, abilities, and even spells as you see fit.

## Combat Encounters

The game master will need to come up with combat encounters for the players to participate in. Each monster will have a given level. When determining the difficulty of a combat encounter, add up the total level of the characters in the combat encounter and the total level of the monsters.

### Prepare for Encounters

It is important to have at the ready all of the player initiative cards that will be needed in the encounter. After character creation, level advancement, or switching weapons, ask the player’s what their initiatives are and have those cards at the ready. Also, have prepared the initiative cards that you expect you will need based on the monsters you expect the character’s will be fighting. You can pre-shuffle this deck and spend a minimal amount of time swapping cards in and out before encounters in order to keep the game moving.

### Easy Encounters

Easy encounters include a total level of the monsters within the encounter to be less than twice as low as the total level of the characters participating in that combat encounter. In this case, the characters should sustain very little damage and should not be challenged.

### Standard Encounters

Standard encounters would include a total level of the monsters within the encounter to be equal to or less than the total level of the characters participating in that combat encounter and more than half of the total level of the characters. In this case, the characters should sustain some damage but be able to confidently overcome the enemy creatures.

### Difficult Encounters

Difficult encounters would include a total level of the monsters within the encounter to be above the total level of the characters and up to twice the total level of the characters. These encounters would significantly drain the characters resources including health, consumables, mana, and heroic actions.

### Diversify Your Encounters

Each character has strengths and weaknesses. Each encounter should in some way target the weaknesses of the characters. This may mean placing monsters in the encounter that target willpower instead of agility, have high initiative to overcome bonus attacks, or cast magic missiles to overcome a high toughness.

### Flow Your Encounters

Each encounter should flow in one way or another into the next encounter. Given the initiative system used in Heroic Adventures, it is very easy to go from one encounter to the next with no setup time. This means that the time between encounters can be quick.

It is advisable to have the enemies that the characters are fighting to push the action as much as the players push the action. Instead of sitting back and waiting for the players to move forward, the enemies might make an attack of their own. In this way a dungeon crawl, or any adventure, can be a push and pull between the characters and their enemies, forcing the action upon each other and keeping the game moving fast.

### Challenge the Players

Make sure that your players cannot defeat every encounter. Make it clear that they need to constantly be on their toes and know when enough is enough, when they are in over their head and need to plan their escape. This play style means that no encounter is routine, and any encounter could escalate into something that they need to escape.

### Escalate Your Encounters

Most encounters should not end the same way they began. Introduce environmental effects that change the terrain. Send in reinforcements for the monsters that might make an easy encounter into a big challenge. The reinforcements could come from different directions, be equipped to target characters who thought they were untouchable or well protected.

### Putting the Pressure On

As the players adventure, they will need to rest and recover before engaging themselves in more dangerous situations. Whether they are resting at an inn or leaving a dungeon to go back to town, these are opportunities for you as the game master to force the players into some tough situations.

When the player characters are vulnerable because of spent mana, low health, and lacking consumables such as health potions and ward stones, you can throw them into an encounter that they were not expecting. Old npcs that they did not treat well might be after them, or whatever enemies they were facing in their adventures might be bringing the action to the players, or some unexpected situation may arise such as the town being attacked by outside forces.

In these situations, the players need to know when to retreat. Sometimes retreating is not an option, and this gives exciting moments to a campaign where the players have to step up and truly take risks in order to save themselves.

### Draining Party Resources

As an adventure unfolds, consider ways to spend party resources before they get to the main encounters that you have planned. If they are searching for an evil wizard, throw some smaller encounters at them first that might require them to spend mana, potions, and ward stones. In this way you make sure that they do not go into every encounter at full strength but must deal with situations where they have been depleted of resources.

### Balancing the Power of Magic

As magic users increase in level, particular wizards, warlocks, druids, and other characters that have access to a lot of mana and spells, it is important to balance the power of these characters with other characters that do not have access to spells. There are a few ways that you can handle this as a game master.

First, do not allow the magic using classes to fight in one encounter, rest up to regain mana, and then fight in one more encounter, and so on. Instead, force situations that require the magic using classes to be careful with how much mana they spend. They might know for example that they are in a dangerous place and so they need to preserve mana and not use it on the first few encounters. Alternatively, when the spell casting characters are low on mana, such as when they are deep in a dungeon after many encounters of combat, getting out of the dungeon requires fighting through more enemies. With the mana depleted, these last few encounters offer opportunities for other characters to shine.

Secondly, consider giving enemies more ward runes to protect them against spell casting characters. Goblins might have ward runes to protect them from spell casting elves, and a necromancer might place ward runes on his skeleton horde. In this way you can dampen the damage output of spell casting classes which allows for other character classes to be more involved.

Alternatively, if you feel that the spell casting characters are not involved enough in the combat and those players are feeling bored, do not give monsters ward runes and allow those players to have more opportunities to affect the combat.

## Creating a Setting

Focus on creating a setting rather than predetermining a story. Allow the players to travel through a world, encounter non playable characters that you have outlined, and face dangers that hide rewards. Also, do not predetermine everything about the setting that you are creating. Allow the players to make some determinations without even knowing it.

You might spend a lot of time outlining the details to a dungeon and the players might decide to go in a completely different direction. This is okay, because you can use the dungeon you created regardless of what ancient door or gaping cave the players decide to walk into. If you need to adjust your setting after the game in order to align with what happened, that means that you are giving players freedom to make their own choices. This is not only expected, but desirable. The same rule goes for non-playable characters.

A setting involves locations and characters. Locations have descriptions and characters have motivations. Once these two things are in place, your setting is ready.

### Creating Non-Playable Characters

When creating non playable characters (npcs), focus on describing their motivations. What do they want and what is their plan for getting it? Make sure that different non playable characters have competing or mutually exclusive motivations. One might want to overthrow the king, and another might want to remain as king. Another might want to overthrow the entire kingdom. Or, on a much smaller scale, a character might want to build a cabin with a small farm but a tribe in the woods wants the woodland to remain as hunting grounds.

As the players interact with these non-playable characters, keep their motivations in mind and allow the npcs to respond to the actions of the players by trying to achieve their goals. This might be staying safe, using the players to overthrow an enemy, and so on. In this way the players will feel like they are interacting with real characters in a developing story that they are helping to create.

### NPCs and Proteges

As you develop the world that the players have adventured in each session, it is important to encourage players to develop relationships to some NPC’s. It might be a local villager who sent them to clear out some goblins from a forest, an innkeeper who they often visit, or any npc that they repeatedly encounter. One way to encourage this is to explain to the player’s that NPCs can become their protégés. In this way, players will be more likely to roleplay and look for opportunities to trust and work with NPCs.

When a player has developed a sufficient relationship with an NPC, allow the player to roll up stats for that NPC and turn them into their own characters protégé. In this way the players can contribute to the game world and play characters that exist in the world, have a history, purpose, and life within the world prior to the player playing them as a character.

### Character Professions

Characters will often need time to rest and recover from their adventures. During this time, the characters will need a place to stay, which can cost them coin. Inns usually cost one coin per character per night. When travelling through unknown lands or in places they otherwise have not been before, this small cost can add up.

When characters are at their hometown or in a place that they have otherwise come to call home, encourage them to use their expertise such as blacksmithing, fishing, or sailing to gain money while not adventuring. This should be a number of coins per day equal to their expertise in the chosen skill. This can cover the costs of an inn but should also require some roleplaying on the part of the players to explain what they are doing to make this coin. This also means that when the players decide to go places, they have never been before or travel abroad, they lose access to this steady stream of income.

Additionally, as the game master, you could introduce NPCs that threaten this stream of income, giving the players motivation to take action.

### Character Retirement

When characters reach level 10, have some epic concluding adventure planned for the players. This could be some adventure that is the culmination of many sessions of play and could dramatically affect the state of the world that the characters are in. After this concluding adventure, the characters that have now reached max level can retire in some way that fits each character.

A wizard might build a tower and live within it studying magic. A fighter might build a training compound where he trains recruits who he leads as a mercenary band. A soldier might become the general to the king. An Alchemist might spend his days creating powerful magical weapons that become legendary artifacts of the game world. Work with each player based upon their character’s role within the game world to determine how they retire.

Any protégé’s of those characters should retire with the main character, allowing the players to enter back into this world as level 1 characters. Their old characters are now key npcs of the world in which they play.

## Creating Locations

When describing locations, provide plenty of details that might interest the players, but be vague, or at least flexible, about the specifics such as the location within the world. If an invading enemy has set up a camp and you want the players to encounter this camp, allow the players to choose where they want to go and then put the camp in the game world wherever it needs to be so that the players encounter it. Give the players hints to act on, and then wherever they go the enemy camp happens to be at that place. The players feel like they followed the clues and discovered it, but their free choice did not ruin the time you spent preparing the adventure. After the game you can update your notes as needed.

Include monster profiles within locations with some sample encounters that you can quickly pull up to throw at the players based upon the actions they take. Have a few easy encounters and moderate encounters in mind such as scouting parties, unprepared encampments of the enemy, guards at an entrance, and the like. Also have in mind some no-win scenario encounters that you can throw at the players. This is a way to drive the players away in order to provide an up and down rhythm to the story and force the players to realize that not every fight is a fight worth having. You could also place some monsters that are far too powerful for the players throughout certain locations, such as a dragon in a cave system that is filled with otherwise easy to defeat monsters. The players will again need to stop and think about which fights are worth having and which need to be avoided.

### Repeat Locations

After creating locations, come up with reasons for why the characters may want to return to the same location multiple times. This allows the players feel like they are getting to know the game world and are not just strung along from one place to the next.

Some ways to do this is to have an inn that they keep coming back to that has familiar names and faces. This inn might have gambling that the characters can participate in or a fight ring that the characters can compete at. There could also be a dungeon complex with multiple competing factions that is a recurring source of problems for the locals, or a recurring opportunity for treasure. Also, when introducing new threats and opportunities for the players to act on, consider involving past locations

Monsters

Monsters are an essential part of Heroic Adventures, providing challenges, adversaries, and opportunities for adventurers. Monsters come in a wide variety of forms, from legendary creatures to common foes, and serve different purposes in the game world.

**Adversaries**: Monsters can serve as adversaries for the player characters, providing challenges and obstacles to overcome. They can be powerful creatures, such as dragons or giants, or smaller foes, such as goblins or skeletons. These encounters can test the players combat skills, strategies, and resource management, adding excitement and tension to the gameplay.

**Environmental Hazards**: Some monsters are not necessarily adversaries in the traditional sense, but rather environmental hazards or obstacles. For example, a treacherous swamp might be home to giant crocodiles or poisonous snakes, or a dense forest might be inhabited by hostile creatures like trolls or sproutlings. These monsters can add depth and complexity to the game world, forcing the players to navigate dangerous terrain and make strategic decisions to avoid or defeat them.

**Role-playing Opportunities**: Monsters can offer rich role-playing opportunities for players, allowing them to interact with unique and memorable characters. For example, a mind slayer or an ocean lord might provide opportunities for diplomacy, negotiation, or creative problem-solving, challenging the players to think beyond combat and use their wits or social skills to overcome obstacles.

**Loot and Rewards**: Monsters often guard valuable treasure, magical items, or rare resources that the players can obtain by defeating them. This can provide a strong incentive for adventurers to seek out and defeat powerful monsters and adds an element of risk versus reward to the gameplay. Monsters can also drop valuable components, such as ether and scroll fragments which can be used for crafting.

**Monster sizes**: Most monsters are roughly the size of a human and take up 1 space on the game board. Other monsters are much bigger, such as ogres, giants, and dragons, and can take up 2 spaces, a 2x2 grid of spaces, or more. It is up to the game master to decide how much space a monster takes up on the board, which is largely dependent on what miniatures you have available.

**Homebrew and Creativity**: One of the joys of tabletop roleplaying is the ability to create your own monsters, either by modifying existing ones or inventing entirely new creatures. This allows the game master and players to exercise their creativity and tailor the game world to their own liking. Homebrew monsters can be used to create unique challenges, puzzles, or encounters that reflect the gamemasters or players imagination and storytelling style.

**Spell casting**: Some monsters have access to mana and spells and will be listed in the monster’s feature list. In this case, monsters can cast spells following the normal rules and can apply one enhancement per monster level.

**Monster strength**: When needing to know a monster’s strength, refer to the static bonus to in their damage stat, ignoring and indicated dice. So, if a monster’s damage is 2d6+5, then the monster’s strength is 5.

## Monster profiles

Monster profiles can be used to quickly determine the stats for a wide range of unique monsters that fit your adventure. Each level range has statistics for each of the monster profiles listed here. This means for low levels, you might use the soldier profile for goblins, the leader profile for the goblin chief, the brute profile for orcs, and the behemoth profile for ogres. At higher levels you might use the minion profile for the same orcs, use the brute profile for ogres, and the leader profile for the ogre chieftain.

### Minion

Minions are weak and fragile creatures that follow the orders of others. They are often used to soak up the damage of opponents and often their only strategy is to use the sheer force of numbers to defeat their enemies.

Examples of minions for low level characters is untrained goblins, spiders, and rats. At higher levels minions could represent orc soldiers or demonic hordes.

### Soldier

Soldiers are creatures trained for war. They fight as a unit, use advanced tactics, and come to the fight prepared with weapons and armor.

Examples of soldiers are trained goblins and orc warriors and town guards.

### Beast

Beasts are creatures that are tough and dangerous, but have low intelligence, do not communicate with other creatures or use tactics, and are generally set lose against enemy combatants but not easily controlled.

Examples of beasts are wolves, giant spiders, alligators, and lions.

### Brute

Brutes are tough and do a lot of damage. They are usually few in number and coordinate with soldiers under the direction of leaders to act as the hammer blow against their enemies.

Examples of brutes at low levels could be orcs and demons. At high levels brutes could be ogres and trolls.

### Behemoth

Behemoths are large creatures that are very tough and do devastating damage. Often there is only or two behemoths in a combat.

At low levels behemoths are things like ogres and trolls. At high levels a behemoth could be a gargantuan spider, drake, or giants.

### Slayer

Slayers are highly skilled and do high damage, but in general are less armored than brutes and soldiers. They are used to stand toe to toe with the best of the enemy combatants.

Examples of slayers are assassins, skilled warriors and ruthless barbarians.

### Commander

Commanders have high stats, provide direction to their allies, and control enemy combatants in various ways in order to manipulate the combat to their advantage.

Examples of leaders are chieftains and captains.

### Leader

Leaders have average stats and are generally vulnerable in combat but often have access to spells and abilities to damage the enemy and help their allies.

Examples of mages are orc shamans and human wizards.

# Mage Spells

## Damage (*minor*)

Choose a creature within 8 spaces and do 1d4 damage that ignores damage reduction

Enhance: Add 1d4 damage

Enhance: Double the range

## Major Damage (*major*)

Choose a creature within 8 spaces and make an arcana vs. willpower attack that does 1d6 + arcana damage that ignores damage reduction.

Enhance: Add 1d6 damage

Enhance: Double the range

## Area Damage (*major*)

Chose a space within 8 spaces. Make an arcana vs. willpower attack against all enemy creatures within 1 space that does 1d6 + arcana damage.

Enhance: Double the distance of the effect from the chosen space

## Control (*minor*)

Choose a space within 8 spaces to place a marker. Enemies that make any move into or out of any space within 2 spaces of the marker have all movements reduced by 1.

## Empower (*minor*)

Choose a space within 8 spaces to place a marker. Allies within 2 spaces of the marker gain advantage to attacks and defense.

# Diviner Spells

## Heal (*minor*)

Heal a target creature within 8 spaces for 1d12 health.

Enhance: Add 1d12 to the healing.

## Protect (*minor*)

Give all allies within 4 spaces advantage to agility and willpower defense rolls.

## Hex (*minor*)

Choose a target enemy creature. All attacks made against that creature until the end of the next round gain bonus damage equal to this the diviners willpower.

## Ward (*minor*)

The next ally within 4 spaces to be damaged by a spell ignores half the damage.
